By Diane M. Ciarloni

Legendary trainer Jack Brooks had a barn crammed with horses when he decided to retire. Horses and their owners always came frst with Brooks, and he wanted to be certain he found excellent “homes” for everyone.

Brooks pulled out his phone and began calling trainer colleagues. One of them was Sleepy Gilbreath.

“He said, ‘Sleepy, you just have to take some of these horses,’” recounted Gilbreath. “I told him I couldn’t handle any more. ‘Besides,’ I said, ‘I couldn’t train for some of the people you train for.’” A few days passed and Brooks called again. This time, he told Sleepy that he had someone he must take.

“He told me they were the best people in the world,” continued Gilbreath. “And I tell you, I’ve never regretted taking them, not even one time. That lady loves her horses and it doesn’t matter which breed.” And that’s how Carolyn and Walt Bay of Michigan (T Bill Stables), along with Cold Cash 123, hooked up with Gilbreath. There’s a picture of her at three on a horse and there’s another one of her at fve, sitting on a horse and watching her grandfather round up cattle. She studied animal science in school and, as far as horses are concerned, she’s “ridden them, showed them, judged

(He won the All American Juvenile two months earlier.) The Bays were thrilled and probably would have been content to stop right there. Cold Cash 123, however, had far bigger ideas.

After the Southwest, Gilbreath decided the bay gelding was ready for a rest, taking him out of action until May 8, 2011, when he fnished ninth in a derby trial at Remington Park. His connections were hoping for a better performance in his frst derby start but his chances fell to zero after being banged around.
